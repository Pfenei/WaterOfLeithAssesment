Welcome to the Water of Leith!

The Water of Leith Walkway is a public footpath and cycleway that runs alongside the small river of the same name through Edinburgh, Scotland, from Balerno to Leith. In the past, the river played a crucial role in developing Edinburgh as an industrial city. Long before the construction of an artificial harbour, the tidal mouth of the river provided a haven for ships, allowing the Port of Leith to flourish as a focus for shipping. Notable imports and exports through Leith included timber, wine, esparto grass for paper and, of course, whisky.

The walkway passes through the historic villages of Balerno, Currie, Juniper Green, Colinton, Slateford, Roseburn, Dean Village, Stockbridge, Canonmills and Warriston, which all developed at places where it was easy to cross the river. Colinton village was founded around 1095. Colinton became part of the City of Edinburgh in 1920. Slateford was built at an ancient river crossing and named after the slate-like rock in the river banks. Slateford House School was converted into the Water of Leith Conservation Trust Visitor Centre, which was completed in 2000. Dean Village was an important mill village. It’s an area full of fascinating industrial heritage, which you can read more about here. Leith is where the Water of Leith enters the River Forth and was once the most important port in Scotland.

Many people who come to visit Edinburgh today aren’t aware of the Water of Leith. Or, if they are, they have no idea just how much beauty and history it has to offer. The Water of Leith is one of Edinburgh’s true treasures, delivering a respite from the hustle and bustle of the city alongside wildlife, beautiful views and historical hamlets. No matter what time of year you choose to explore the river, it is always guaranteed to replenish and calm. It is just one more example of what makes Edinburgh unique as a UK city.
